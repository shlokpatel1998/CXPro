## 1. The market is small at the core but adjacent to several massive pools

### Pure-play commissioning software is a $1.35B → $3.8B niche

The most defensible figure for **dedicated construction commissioning management software** is **~$1.35B globally in 2024**, growing at **~12.1% CAGR to ~$3.77B by 2033** (Growth Market Reports). North America is roughly **38% (~$520M)**, Western Europe ~$410M, APAC ~$280M, Middle East $80–150M but the fastest growing on a Saudi Vision 2030 / NEOM tailwind. The underlying **commissioning services market** that pays for software is **~$3.9–6.4B globally** depending on definition (Mordor / Custom Market Insights / Navigant legacy), growing 6.4–6.7% — meaning **software is growing roughly 2x faster than services**, the classic vertical SaaS pattern.

Several second-tier reports cite $14–44B for "engineering and commissioning software," but those conflate BIM, CAD, and PLM. **Use $1.35B / 12% / $3.8B by 2033 when pitching VCs** — it is the only defensible number that is specifically "construction Cx software."

## 2. The competitive landscape has a clear, attackable seam

### The incumbents are small, old, or distracted

**CxAlloy** is the market-share leader and runs on the Microsoft / Turner & Townsend hyperscaler builds, but it is bootstrapped, ~<50 employees, and ships no real AI — just an OCR feature for equipment labels. The pricing is per-project with unlimited users, which makes them defensively sticky with Cx firms but **structurally unable to capture the value of a 10,000-asset hyperscaler program**.

**CxPlanner** (Denmark, founded 2020) is the most aggressive AI-positioned incumbent — they market "CxAI Agents" and a dedicated data center page — but the team is 15–30 people, US presence is nascent, and their recent investment is from a small family office (Compounding Capital). They are vulnerable to a US-anchored, hyperscaler-native challenger.

**BlueRithm** rebuilt their product as "Bluerithm 2.0" in late 2025 and explicitly designed it to treat **agentic AI tools like Claude Computer Use and Perplexity Computer Use as first-class users** via an MCP server — the most credible AI-native pivot in the category. **This is the single fastest-moving competitor and the one to benchmark against weekly.** Per-seat pricing keeps them in mid-market territory.

**Facility Grid** took private equity from **Nexa Equity in August 2025** and hired Daniel Russo (ex-JLL Building Engines CEO) — they are now rolling up into an owner-side / lifecycle Cx play, but with only 18 employees they cannot ship faster than a focused two-founder team for at least 18 months.

**Hexagon Smart Completions / Coreworx C3D ATLAS** dominates LNG, mining, and Aramco-class EPC commissioning but the UX is legacy enterprise — unusable for hyperscaler iteration speed.

**InEight** is Kiewit-owned and explicitly "not recommended for Asset Owners or Client Side PMs" per third-party analysts (Mastt).

### The platform consolidators will not chase the deep workflow

Procore's January 2026 Datagrid acquisition (terms undisclosed) and the hire of Thiago da Costa to lead AI strategy is the biggest signal — Procore is going wide on agentic AI for submittals/RFIs across the *entire* construction system of record, not deep on commissioning. Autodesk shipped basic Handover and Forms-in-Handover features in 2024–2025 plus the Rhumbix acquisition (March 2026), but Autodesk's DNA is architect/engineer, not functional testing. **Neither platform will build hyperscaler Level 1–5 commissioning workflows natively** — they will buy the eventual winner at 8–11x ARR, the same multiple range Autodesk paid for PlanGrid ($875M at ~$100M ARR ≈ 8.75x) and Bentley paid for Seequent (~11x ARR).

### Where AI is genuinely working versus where it is marketing

It is **working** for reality-capture progress tracking (Buildots, OpenSpace, DroneDeploy's Progress AI claiming 95%+ accuracy on hyperscale projects), autonomous HVAC control (BrainBox AI, acquired by Trane for ~$70–100M raised), and contract review (Document Crunch, acquired by Trimble April 2026). It is **marketing veneer** for AI-generated checklists (CxPlanner, BlueRithm, flowdit all claim it with little behavior change) and the LLM chatbots bolted onto legacy CDEs (Autodesk Assistant, Oracle, Hexagon). **No vendor has yet shipped an AI agent that owns the full Level 1 factory witness → Level 5 integrated systems testing loop with structured-data outputs that feed the digital twin.** That is the unclaimed territory.

### Seven attackable white spaces, ranked

1. **Hyperscale data center Cx workflow native** — Buildots/OpenSpace do progress tracking, not L1–L5 functional testing; CxPlanner has marketing pages but no real install base at this scale. **Highest-value gap.**
2. **Owner-side continuous Cx with closed-loop AI agents** — Clockworks, Switch, KODE all do FDD analytics that *tell* you what is wrong but do not *execute* the fix. PassiveLogic is closest but hardware-dependent.
3. **10,000+ asset Cx scale** — Pure plays optimized for <500 asset buildings; the AWS/Haskell Mississippi project (9,000+ assets) was built on a custom Exto platform — a tell.
4. **Pharma CQV bridge** — Cx + IQ/OQ/PQ is the same workflow; Kneat and ValGenesis don't speak construction-Cx language; no vendor crosses the bridge.
5. **Healthcare / hospital Cx with Joint Commission, ASHRAE 170, NFPA 99 templates** — Clockworks has Kaiser but FDD-only; no pure-play owns OR/imaging/negative-pressure room Cx.
6. **Semiconductor fab Cx** — Buildots has Intel as a customer but for progress tracking only; cleanroom IQ/OQ requires NEBB Cleanroom certification and ISO 14644 logic no Cx vendor ships.
7. **Middle East / GCC Vision 2030, NEOM, Qiddiya, Red Sea, ADQ** — Novade + PlanRadar have field presence but not Cx specialization; Aramco/Novade/WakeCap shows the regional appetite.

## 3. Pricing and ARR benchmarks favor a hybrid model anchored on enterprise

### Pricing models in the field

Construction software pricing splits cleanly by buyer scale. **SMB and mid-market** (Cx firms, mid-size GCs) pay **per-seat ~$30–$200/user/month** (PlanRadar published rates: $32 basic, $107 starter, $159 pro; SafetyCulture $24/user/month; BlueRithm per-seat with multi-year discounts). **Enterprise** (Procore, OpenSpace, Buildots) prices off **Annual Construction Volume** at roughly **0.1–0.2% of hard construction cost — about $500–$1,000 per $1M of construction value**. CxAlloy and CxPlanner use private quote-based pricing with per-project ranges typically **$15K–$60K per Cx firm annually** and **$200K–$500K per enterprise GC**.

For a hyperscale-anchored AI-native Cx platform, the **right pricing structure is a base subscription plus a per-project or per-MW commissioning fee**, modeled on the OpenSpace / Buildots enterprise tier. Buildots reportedly signed **multiple 7-figure deals in 2025**.

### Average contract values by customer type

- **Small/mid Cx firm (10–50 employees):** $15K–$60K/yr — avoid as primary GTM, low ACV and low strategic value
- **Mid-market GC ($10–100M revenue):** $40K–$100K/yr
- **Enterprise GC (Turner, DPR, Mortenson, JE Dunn, Whiting-Turner):** **$250K–$2M/yr**
- **Building owner (REIT/university/health system):** $50K–$500K/yr
- **Hyperscaler (Google, Microsoft, AWS, Meta, Oracle, Tesla):** **$1M–$10M+/yr per program**, modeled on Intel's combined customer+investor relationship with Buildots

## 4. The optimal wedge: hyperscaler data center Cx in Texas, then Ontario hospital P3s

### Why hyperscale data centers first, not SMB Cx firms

The instinct for a bootstrapped team is to sell to the customers the 10 advisor experts know — small Cx firms paying $15K–$60K per year. **Resist that instinct.** That motion takes the same sales effort as a $500K hyperscaler pilot, produces 30x less revenue, and creates a feature set optimized for the wrong buyer. The hyperscale wedge has four properties no other segment has: (1) cost-of-delay is $14M+/month, so ROI is trivial to prove, (2) two or three logos make the company, (3) the work is repetitive across 8–24 nearly-identical buildings per campus, so each contract compounds, and (4) the buyers are sophisticated technical operators who will buy from a 2-person team if the product is real.

### The MVP: a Cx agent operating system, not another checklist app

Build, in this order over 90 days:

1. **A structured commissioning data model** for Level 0 design review through Level 5 integrated systems testing, with first-class objects for asset, system, test procedure, witness, deviation, and turnover package. This is the schema CxAlloy lacks at scale.
2. **An AI agent layer** — initially three agents: a *Test Procedure Generator* that ingests OPR, BOD, and submittals and outputs draft L3/L4/L5 procedures; a *Deviation Triage Agent* that classifies issues against the test plan and routes them to the responsible sub; and a *Handover Package Compiler* that assembles the L5 dossier in days, not weeks. Hexagon Smart Completions reported 98% reduction in dossier compile time at an Australian LNG project, which is the headline benchmark.
3. **Hyperscaler-grade scale primitives** — sub-second query on 10,000+ assets, mobile offline mode for crews of 1,500+, sequential test gating, QR-code asset tracking, real-time multi-sub concurrency.
4. **MCP server + agent-friendly API** so Claude/Perplexity/Cursor can drive the system. This is exactly the wedge BlueRithm 2.0 just announced — match it within 60 days.
5. **Procore and Autodesk Construction Cloud integrations** — be the depth layer above their breadth, the way OpenSpace lives in the Procore Marketplace.

Avoid for the first 12 months: BIM authoring, 3D progress capture (let DroneDeploy / Buildots own that and integrate), continuous Cx (a different buyer), and FedRAMP authorization (return to it for the Ontario federal play in year two).

### The pricing model

A three-tier structure works:

- **Project tier:** $75K–$250K per data center building, recognized as ARR via multi-year master agreements
- **Campus tier:** $1–3M annual subscription covering an entire hyperscaler campus
- **Enterprise tier:** $3–10M annual platform fee plus 0.05% of construction volume for a hyperscaler signing across all North American builds

Per-seat is wrong here; this is mission-critical software where buyers want unlimited users. Per-square-foot is wrong because hyperscalers measure in MW, not sqft. The OpenSpace/Buildots ACV approach is the correct mental model.

## 5. Strategic risks and how to defuse them

### Procore / Autodesk consolidation is a feature, not a bug

The platform consolidators will not build deep Level 1–5 hyperscaler Cx workflows — they will buy. The single greatest risk is signaling acquisition desire too early — keep the door open but build for $100M ARR.

### Capital strategy: bootstrap to $1M ARR, raise selectively from $1M

With 2 technical founders, 10 domain advisors, and the customer profile above, the capital efficiency case is strong. Realistic monthly burn is $30–60K through month 12 if the founders defer salaries against pilot deposits. ### The IP moat is the schema and the agent loop, not the LLM

LLMs are commodities. The defensible moat is (a) the **structured commissioning data ontology** that maps OPR/BOD/test procedure/deviation/turnover to industry standards (ASHRAE Guideline 0, Standard 202, NEBB, ACG, BCxA, LEED v4.1 EAp1+EAc, GSA P100, DoD UFC 1-200-02, FDA 21 CFR Part 11, GAMP 5), (b) the **closed-loop agent system** that learns from field outcomes across hundreds of projects, and (c) **proprietary integrations** into the major BMS/EMS/CMMS systems (Siemens Desigo, Honeywell Niagara, Johnson Controls Metasys, Schneider EcoStruxure, IBM Maximo, Accruent, Nuvolo). File provisional patents on the agent-driven test generation loop and on the structured handover dossier compilation method — the Hexagon LNG-style "98% reduction in dossier compile time" claim is patentable if grounded in a specific method.
